# Quest Engine Status — 2026-04-14

## What we're trying to build

A **hierarchical narrative generation engine** for text quests — interactive
fiction where readers vote on what the protagonist does and the system
generates the next chapter of prose in response. The engine has to:

1. Maintain consistent characters, world rules, themes, and motifs across
   hundreds of chapters.
2. Plan at multiple temporal scales: campaign arc → chapter → scene → prose.
3. Honor reader-supplied actions while serving longer-arc plot goals.
4. Render prose in a controlled style (specific narrator voice, character
   POV, register), not the generic "AI assistant" voice models default to.
5. Run fast enough for production loops (target: 30+ candidate chapters per
   minute via vllm), so we can generate many candidates and pick the best.

The backbone is a **planner stack** (arc → dramatic → emotional → craft →
write → check → extract) where each layer makes specific decisions and
hands a structured plan to the next. The bet is that with good planners,
even a small model (LFM2.5-1.2B) can produce prose competitive with what
larger models do unguided.

## Why each thing on the roadmap matters

### Why the gap analysis (G1-G14) was urgent

Without these, the planner stack lacked the persistent state needed for
multi-chapter coherence. Specifically:

- **Narrator (G1)** — without a persistent narrator definition, every
  chapter the LLM picks a slightly different voice. Reader notices
  immediately ("this chapter feels different"). Now there's a single
  Narrator object loaded into every craft plan.
- **Themes (G4) + motifs (G5) + parallels (G12)** — these are how
  literary fiction creates resonance. Without persistence, the LLM
  forgets the theme it was developing 5 chapters ago.
- **Reader model (G6)** — the dramatic planner needs to know what
  questions are still open in the reader's mind, what expectations are
  set, how long since the last major event. Without this, every chapter
  plans in isolation and the rhythm goes flat.
- **Information asymmetry (G7)** — dramatic irony, mystery, suspense
  all depend on tracking who-knows-what. Without it the engine can
  never set up "the reader knows X but the character doesn't" moments.
- **Voice grounding (G3, G9, G10, G13)** — these tie the writer's
  craft instructions to specific character/narrator data instead of
  the LLM inventing them per scene. The improvement over baseline is
  the difference between "Aldric notices the door" (generic) and
  "Aldric's eyes go to the door — old habit, sergeant's eye, exits
  first" (grounded in his stored detail/perception profile).

12 of 14 closed; G11 (temporal richness) and G14 (specialized critics)
deferred because their absence isn't currently the bottleneck.

### Why the calibration corpus

We need an **objective measure of prose quality** to know if changes to
the planner stack help or hurt. Eyeballing 3 candidates is fine for
debugging; for systematic improvement we need scores across many
dimensions on many passages.

The corpus is **literature scored by Claude-Opus** on 9 dimensions
(free-indirect-quality, interiority depth, sensory density, etc.).
Claude is the highest-quality scorer we can get without paying for
human raters.

We curated a corpus of 195 mid-chapter passages from 13 works spanning:
- **High end**: Joyce, Woolf-via-related-text, Ulysses, Karamazov, Bovary
- **Mid**: Austen, Hemingway, Le Guin, Pratchett, Wodehouse, Jerome
- **Quest fiction**: Marked for Death, Pale Lights, A Practical Guide to Evil
- **Sanderson Way of Kings Prime** (early draft, intentionally rougher)

Plus 65 scene-scale (~3000 word) excerpts for arc-level dimension scoring
where 800-word passages can't capture (tension arc, scene coherence).

This corpus is the **ground truth** for everything else: for tuning
prompts, training scorers, validating retrieval, and A/B testing changes.

### Why finetune a scorer?

Two reasons:
1. **Inference cost**. Calling Claude on every candidate during rerank
   would cost a fortune. We need a local scorer that runs on the same
   GPU as the writer.
2. **Production rerank**. G2's generate-N rerank works only as well as
   the scoring function. The current scoring uses heuristic critics
   (does the prose mention the named entities? does POV stay consistent?
   is the sensory distribution roughly right?). Those catch obvious
   failures but can't distinguish "good prose" from "competent prose".
   A learned scorer could.

We tried finetuning LFM2.5-1.2B as both an absolute scorer (output
0-1 per dim) and a pairwise judge (output A or B). 

**The pairwise approach failed** at 53% accuracy (random = 50%).
LFM-1.2B simply can't do the literary comparison task — even after
finetuning on 4800 training pairs it overfit and didn't generalize.
This tells us either we need a much bigger base model (Qwen-9B, Gemma-27B)
or we need to give up on learned subjective scoring and stick with
heuristics + retrieval-based grounding.

**The absolute approach partially worked** — sensory_density got to
r=0.72 vs Claude (clears the >0.7 threshold), and overall correlation
hit 0.42 (vs 0.25 baseline). The structural dims (sentence variance,
dialogue ratio) are fine via heuristics. But the subjective dims (FIS,
interiority, subtext) still need help.

**Current decision:** stop trying to make LFM a literary judge.
Heuristics + retrieval (next section) gets us further than throwing
more LoRA at the problem.

### Why retrieval is the load-bearing next thing

The story-gen demo on April 13 produced this prose for "I study the
room, looking for who's trying too hard not to be noticed":

> *"You traced the edge of the table, fingers brushing the edge where
> the light hit just so. The room hummed with a quiet tension, a breath
> held between shadows..."*

This is the failure mode that motivates retrieval: the LLM defaults to
**"literary cliché soup"** — mood phrases that signal "literary fiction"
without doing any specific work. "Hummed with a quiet tension". "A breath
held between shadows". You could substitute these into any scene.

Why? The model has no concrete examples of what *good prose for THIS
specific scene* looks like. The prompt tells it the narrator's pov_type
and the scene's emotional intensity, but those abstractions don't pull
strong enough writing out of a 1.2B model. The model needs **examples**.

The retrieval layer's job: **at write-time, find 3-5 passages from our
labeled corpus that match the current scene's POV, target voice
distinctiveness, target emotion, etc., and inject them as few-shot
anchors in the write prompt.** The model now has concrete prose to match
the rhythm and texture of, instead of inventing "literary mood" from scratch.

Plus the engine needs **continuity retrieval** — pulling earlier
chapters of the same quest that mention the entities currently on screen,
so the prose can naturally callback to past events without us having to
manually pass them in.

## What "retrieval issues" actually means

When the previous status said "failure mode that motivates retrieval"
without context — that meant the prose problems above. Concretely:

- **Cliché density**: phrases like "weight settle in your bones",
  "consequence unfurled", "hummed with quiet tension" appearing 3-5
  times per 800-word passage. Industry baseline cliché count for
  literary fiction is ~0-1 per 800 words.
- **POV drift**: narrator config says `third_limited` but prose comes
  out second-person. Likely a default-quest-POV override; need to fix.
- **Voice generic-ness**: the narrator object specifies "weathered
  observer who notices hands and silences" with a sample of "She set
  the cup down the way she did everything else — like the cup owed
  her rent." The prose ignored both — voice was indistinguishable
  from a generic literary narrator.
- **No callbacks**: chapter 2 mentioned the Gannet crew (action input);
  chapter 3 didn't reference it again. Without retrieval there's no
  signal pulling earlier-chapter material forward into context.
- **Empty choices**: dramatic planner emitted suggested_choices, but
  they didn't render — likely a serialization bug downstream.

These are fixable individually but they're symptoms of a larger pattern:
**the writer is generating in a near-vacuum, without enough concrete
context to ground its choices**. Retrieval addresses that pattern at
its source.

  embeddings (off until Wave 3b activates it)

**Wave 2 (writer integration + semantic) ✓**
- `app/retrieval/passage_retriever.py`: literary-corpus retriever.
  Metadata-only mode (filters: pov, is_quest, score_ranges,
  exclude_works) and hybrid mode (50/50 metadata + semantic cosine)
- `app/retrieval/craft_retriever.py`: wraps existing CraftLibrary
  with metadata filter (tool_id, scale)
- `app/engine/pipeline.py::_run_write`: per-scene `Query` builds
  filters from craft + emotional plan, retrieves k=3 voice anchors
- `prompts/stages/write/user.j2`: renders "VOICE ANCHORS — passages
  to match in style and rhythm" block above the prose brief
- All gated by `quest_config["retrieval"]["enabled"]` flag, default
  off — strict regression of existing pipelines

**Wave 3 (quest retrieval + dramatic) ✓**
- `app/retrieval/quest_retriever.py`: per-quest retriever reading
  `narrative_embeddings`. Required `seed_text`; optional filters:
  `entity_mentions` (1.5x boost), `max_updates_ago`, `last_n_records`
- `app/retrieval/scene_retriever.py`: `SceneShapeRetriever` for
  arc-scale exemplars (reads `scenes_manifest.yaml` + arc labels)
- Extract-stage embedder activated; commits now write per-scene
  embeddings into the quest db
- Writer prompt now also injects "IN-QUEST CALLBACKS" block from
  QuestRetriever
- DramaticPlanner accepts `scene_retriever`; injects scene-shape
  exemplars into the dramatic prompt

## Open questions

- **Default retrieval flag**: should we flip `retrieval.enabled = true`
  in the quest config defaults once Wave 4 lands and the A/B looks
  good? Currently strictly opt-in.
- **Embedding model**: stay on MiniLM (22MB, fast, OK quality) or
  graduate to BGE-small (~120MB, better at literary text but more
  GPU)? Worth A/B-ing once we have measured prose-quality deltas.
- **Pairwise scorer abandoned forever?** It failed on LFM-1.2B but
  could work on a 9B base. Decision deferred until we know whether
  retrieval-augmented prose alone is good enough for production.
