The air in the Azulejo hostel tasted of stale sweat and cheap tallow, heavy enough to coat the tongue. Tristan moved through the dark, his feet finding the floorboards by memory rather than sight. He was a shadow among shadows, a rat slipping through the cracks of a sleeping house.

The ticking in his head was frantic, a staccato rhythm of near-misses and bad odds. *Too fast,* he thought. *The house is winning.*

"Quiet, you little gutter-rat," Fortuna whispered. She was sprawled mid-air, her red dress billowing as if she were underwater, her golden eyes fixed on the heavy breathing coming from the center of the room. "He’s a big one. A mountain of a man. If he wakes, he won't just bite; he'll crush."

Tristan didn't look at her. He knew she was there, a shimmering weight against his shoulder, but looking at a goddess when you were trying to rob a Watchman was a good way to end up with a knife in your ribs. The man, Yaotl, lay beneath a half-folded black cloak. Every exhale was a low, rhythmic rumble that vibrated through the floorboards and up into Tristan’s heels. 

Tristan knelt before the small ironwood cabinet. His hands, usually unhurried, felt the tremor of the ticking. The odds were leaning toward a disaster.

He pulled a thin strip of steel from his belt. The lock was old, stubborn, and biting back. *Click.* Too loud. Tristan froze. He held his breath until his lungs burned, listening.

Yaotl shifted. The heavy man groaned, the sound of grinding stone, and the cloak rustled like a landslide. Tristan squeezed his eyes shut, praying to a goddess who mostly just caused trouble.

"Don't pouts at him," Tristan muttered under his breath, his voice a mere ghost of a sound. "He's just a man."

"He's a man with a sword!" Fortuna countered, her voice a theatrical hiss. She sat cross-legged on the edge of the cabinet, her tiny feet dangling over the wood. "And you're a boy with a piece of tin. Fix it. Tip the scales."

Tristan reached into his pouch and withdrew a small vial of Spinster's Milk. He let a single, viscous drop fall onto his thumb. The liquid was cool and numbing; it steadied the micro-tremors in his fingers, turning his nerves into something as calm as a deep well. 

He slid the pick back into the keyhole. He felt the tumblers—the teeth of the lock—grinding against the steel. He leaned into the sensation, reading the metal as if it were a deck of cards. *One, two, a heavy hitch... and there.*

The lock gave way with a soft, muffled *thunk*. 

The cabinet door creaked—a tiny, agonizing sound—but Yaotl only let out a long, whistling sigh and settled deeper into his blankets. The mountain had moved, but it had not woken.

Tristan’s heart hammered against his ribs, a trapped bird in a cage of bone. He reached into the dark maw of the cabinet, his fingers brushing against the edge of the parchment. He gripped it, feeling the dry, expensive texture of the paper, and pulled.

"Quickly, quickly!" Fortuna urged, leaning so far forward she nearly tumbled into the wood. "The luck is turning sour!"

He gathered the papers into a tight bundle, tucking them against his chest. He didn't linger to see if there was gold or silver; the papers were the prize, and the prize was heavy enough. He backed away, his movements a liquid retreat, until his spine met the cold stone of the wall. 

Yaotl breathed again—a deep, satisfied sound of a man dreaming of easy victories. Tristan waited, a shadow anchored to the floor, watching the golden girl of the odds settle back into the rafters to watch him.
