The Azulejo hostel smelled of old salt, unwashed linen, and the slow, sour rot of the Estebra District. Tristan moved through the gloom not as a man, but as a shadow attempting to mimic the dark. The air was heavy, a humid weight that pressed against his skin, making the fine hairs on his arms stand in anticipation.

*Left foot, soft. Right foot, silent.*

Inside his skull, the ticking began. It wasn't a sound, exactly, but a rhythmic, frantic pulse that sat behind his eyes—the internal metronome of Fortuna. She was sprawled somewhere in the rafters above him, her molten gold hair dangling down like weeping willow strands, her red dress a splash of impossible heat against the damp wood. She wasn't looking at the prize; she was watching the odds. And right now, the odds were screaming.

*Click-tick. Click-tick.*

Tristan froze. Beside him, the massive frame of Yaotl Cuatzo shifted. The Aztlan man was a mountain of sleeping muscle, his broad chin resting against a folded black cloak. A heavy, wet snore tore from his throat, a sound so violent Tristan felt it in his own marrow. He held his breath, his lungs burning, waiting for the vibration to shake the very floorboards beneath his toes.

*One in ten,* Fortuna whispered in the tilt of his mind. *Maybe one in twelve.*

He waited until the snore subsided into a low, rhythmic rumble. Slowly, Tristan reached for the wooden cabinet beside Yaotl’s trunk. His fingers, usually so unhurried and polite, felt like frantic spiders. He could feel the sweat slicking his palms, a traitorous moisture that threatened to dull the precision of his touch.

He gripped the lock of the cabinet. It was a stubborn piece of ironwork, notched and uneven. Tristan drew a thin strip of steel from his belt—a tool he knew as well as his own teeth—and began to probe the keyway.

*Click.*

The sound was microscopic, a mere ghost of a noise, but in the heavy silence of the hostel, it sounded to Tristan like a hammer striking an anvil. He went rigid. 

Yaotl groaned. The man’s hand, calloused and dark, drifted toward the hilt of the arming sword resting atop his trunk. The movement was lazy, the heavy sleep of a soldier, but to Tristan, it was a landslide. He squeezed his eyes shut, praying to the goddess of long odds, his heart hammering a frantic, uneven rhythm against his ribs that seemed loud enough to wake the entire district.

*Tick-tick-tick-tick.*

*Stay down,* Tristan thought, his gaze fixed on the curve of Yaotl’s shoulder. *Stay in the deep dark, big man.*

The snoring leveled out. The hand slumped back onto the cloak. Tristan let out a breath that was more a shudder than a sigh. His nerves were raw, frayed like an old rope under tension. He needed to steady himself, or the sheer vibration of his own terror would give him away.

Reaching into a small pouch at his waist, his fingers brushed against a tiny, translucent vial. He pulled it out with the delicacy of a man handling a bird’s egg. Spinster’s Milk. He uncorked it with a thumb-flick so slight it didn't even disturb the air, and let a single, cold drop fall onto the tip of his tongue.

The liquid was sharp, a numbing frost that raced down his throat and settled in his chest. It quieted the frantic 'ticking' in his head, smoothing the jagged edges of his pulse. The world didn't slow down, but Tristan did. His hands, once trembling, grew heavy and sure.

He returned to the lock. This time, he didn't fight the metal; he danced with it. He felt the tumblers, the tiny, stubborn teeth of the mechanism, and coaxed them into place with the practiced grace of a man born to the Murk.

*Snap.*

The lock gave way. The sound was a tiny, triumphant click, felt more through his fingertips than heard through the air. The cabinet door creaked open a hair’s breadth. 

Tristan lunged forward, his movement a blur of controlled desperation. He reached into the dark belly of the cabinet and found them: the stack of parchment, tucked away like a secret whispered in a crowd. The paper felt dry and brittle beneath his touch, a precious weight of knowledge and gold.

As he slid the papers from their resting place, a sudden, heavy breath erupted from the bed beside him. It was a deep, lung-filling inhalation from Yaotl, a sound of a man shifting from a shallow sleep into something more alert.

Tristan didn't wait. He tucked the parchment against his chest, his fingers curling around the edges as if he could merge with the paper, and retreated into the velvet shadows of the corner. He stayed there, a ghost among the dust motes, listening to the heavy, rhythmic breathing of the giant, his heart still racing even as the Spinster's Milk cooled his blood. He had the prize, but the air still felt too thick, and the dark felt far too thin.

The alleyway tasted of damp stone and old cooking fat, a cool, dark reprieve from the frantic, salt-sweat heat of the hostel. Tristan slumped against a wall of soot-stained brick, his breath coming in ragged, uneven pulls that rattled in his chest. He waited for the world to stop spinning, for the phantom sensation of the heist—the rush of blood, the scrape of leather on cobblestone—to settle into his bones.

He pulled the stolen bundle from his tunic. The parchment was soft, almost oily to the touch, crinkling with a sound like dry leaves in a gutter. He spread them across a flat, uneven slab of granite that served as a makeshift table, his fingers trembling just enough to make the edges dance.

"Quiet down," he muttered to his hands, though whether he was speaking to his muscles or the goddess sitting on the edge of the stone, he wasn't sure.

Fortuna was there, of course. She was sprawled across the granite slab, her molten gold hair spilling over the edges of the papers like liquid sunlight. She wore a dress the color of a bruised pomegranate, and right now, she was leaning forward, her chin propped on her palm, peering at the ink with an expression of intense, divine boredom.

"They look like nothing," Fortuna said, her voice a shimmering chime that only Tristan could hear. She reached out a translucent finger to poke a smudge of black verity, but her hand passed through the paper without a ripple. "Just squiggles and stains, Tristan. A waste of a good run. We could be eating."

Tristan ignored her. He lit a small tallow candle, shielding the flame with a calloused palm. The light was dim, a pale amber eye blinking in the dark, but it was enough. He leaned in, his eyes tracing the lines.

At first, it was the usual clutter of a merchant’s ledger: columns of numbers, names of ships, weights of spice. But as he squinted, the patterns began to shift. The ink wasn't just spilled; it was deliberate. He saw a series of small, repeated marks—triangles with a dot in the center. *Gongmin locks.* Not the metal kind he carried at his belt, but a cipher used by the high-tier guilds to denote secure transit.

His thumb traced a particularly dark blotch near the margin. It wasn't a blotch. It was a seal, partially obscured by a wash of Spinster’s Milk. He wiped the surface gingerly with a corner of his sleeve.

The seal resolved: a single, serrated leaf. A *Hoja Roja*.

Tristan felt a sudden, sharp chill that had nothing to do with the night air. The man he had slit in the shadow of the granary—the man whose blood had stained Tristan’s knuckles and whose life had slipped away with a wet, surprised grunt—hadn't been a random traveler or a drunken clerk. He had been a leaf of the Red Sheet. An agent.

"A leaf," Fortuna whispered, her golden eyes widening. She sat up straight, her boredom vanishing. "A red leaf in a dark forest. You didn't just steal paper, Tristan. You stole a secret."

Tristan didn't answer. He was looking at the way the lines connected. The trade routes didn't just flow toward the coast; they converged. Every major artery of ink, every debt and every shipment marked with that crimson leaf, bled toward a single point of interest.

He traced a line from the Estebra District, following a winding path of numbers that grew larger, more frantic, until they hit a name written in a hand so precise it looked carved: *The Bluebell*.

The Watch’s ferry. The vessel that moved between the layers of the city, the one thing that connected the gutter to the heights without ever touching the ground.

A heavy, thudding sensation bloched out the sound of the distant city—a physical blow to his solar plexus. He sat back, the granite biting into his thighs. 

This wasn't luck. Tristan lived by the odds; he knew when the dice were rolling in his favor, and he knew when the goddess was simply being kind. But this? This was too clean. The theft had been easy—too easy. The man had been positioned exactly where the light hit the alley. The papers had been tucked exactly where a reaching hand would find them.

"Abuela," he breathed.

The name felt heavy on his tongue, like a coin made of lead.

He saw it then: the invisible strings. Every job she had given him for the last three months, every 'lucky' find in a merchant's pocket, every little errand that seemed to lead nowhere—they were all breadcrumbs. She hadn't been teaching him to be a thief; she had been training him to be a navigator. She had been guiding his hands, his eyes, his very curiosity, toward this specific scrap of parchment, this specific name, this specific moment of realization.

He wasn't just following a map of the city. He was following her will. And her will was a straight, unwavering line that led directly to the Bluebell.

"She's playing us," Tristan said, a bitter, jagged laugh escaping his throat. He looked up at the rafters of the alleyway, where Fortuna was now pacing, her red dress swirling like smoke. "She's been playing the whole game since before we even knew the board was set."

"She's a genius," Fortuna said, beaming, her golden eyes glowing with reflected candlelight. "And you're a very good rat."

Tristan looked back at the papers. The resentment flickered in his chest—a sharp, gutter-born heat. He hated being steered. He hated the feeling of being a piece on a board, moved by hands he couldn't see. He was a man of the Murk; he liked to own his own luck. 

But as he stared at the ink, at the way the *Hoja Roja* seal seemed to pulse in the dim light, the resentment softened into something more dangerous: purpose. 

The papers weren't just a job. They were the map to his vendetta. Every line of ink was a step closer to the thing that had been hollowed out of his life, and Abuela had handed him the key without ever having to say a word.

He reached out and closed his hand over the parchment, gripping it tight. The paper was cold, but the truth of it burned.

"Fine," he whispered to the dark, to the goddess, and to the old woman who sat at the center of the web. "Fine. We go to the Bluebell."

The shadow of the Estebra District gave way to a roar of light and brine. Tristan burst from the narrow, weeping alleyways of the Murk into the Sacromonte Docks, and the world suddenly became too much—too loud, too bright, too wide. The air here was a thick soup of salt, rotting kelp, and the sharp, oily tang of pine resin, all churned by a wind that tasted of the deep sea.

He ran. His boots, worn thin at the soles, slapped against the slick cobblestones and the uneven timber of the piers. Around him, the docks were a riot of motion: sailors with skin like cured leather shouting orders in gutter-tongues, long-tailed gulls screaming overhead, and the relentless, rhythmic groaning of massive timber hulls straining against their moorings. 

*Faster, you little rat,* he thought, his lungs beginning to burn with the salt air. *The tide waits for no man, and Fortuna loves a man who is late.*

In the corner of his eye, a flash of molten gold danced atop a stack of fish crates. Fortuna was sprawling there, her red dress a vibrant wound against the grey wood, her chin resting in her palms as she watched him scramble. She looked bored, as she often did when the odds were stacked against him, but her golden eyes were fixed on the horizon.

"Don't look at me like that," Tristan hissed, sidestepping a porter carrying a crate of silver-scaled sea bass. "I'm coming."

"You're stumbling," the goddess remarked, her voice a melodic chime that only he could hear. She kicked a loose pebble with a foot that had no weight, yet the pebble skittered as if struck. "A man who stumbles is a man who loses his footing before the dance even begins."

Tristan ignored her, though the 'ticking' in his head—that rhythmic, internal pulse of probability—was accelerating. *Click-click-click.* The rhythm was frantic.

He crested the final rise of the quay, and there she was. The *Bluebell*. 

The ship was a magnificent, terrifying thing of white sail and dark oak, already straining against its heavy hempen ropes. It sat low in the water, its masts reaching for the sky like the fingers of a drowning giant. The crew was a blur of motion, hauling lines and kicking away the last of the mooring stones. The ship was preparing to catch the tide, to vanish into the open sea and leave the safety of the harbor behind.

Tristan slowed his pace, his chest heaving, his eyes scanning the chaotic swarm of passengers boarding the gangplank. He was a creature of the shadows, a thin, dark-haired boy of the Estebra, used to the quiet corners where things were hidden. But here, under the vastness of the sky, he felt small—until he saw her.

High above the common bustle of merchants and sailors, a figure moved with a grace that seemed to defy the uneven pitch of the deck. Angharad Tredegar. 

Even from this distance, Tristan recognized the silhouette of House Tredegar—the straight back, the regal carriage of a woman who had been born to command. She was dressed for travel, but there was a tension in her shoulders that spoke of something more than a mere voyage. She moved through the crowd like a blade through silk, her fluid grace cutting through the clumsy jostle of the dockworkers. She looked less like a noblewoman embarking on a grand journey and more like a predator in flight, her dark skin gleaming under the midday sun, her eyes scanning the docks with a piercing, wary intelligence.

Tristan leaned against a salt-crusted bollard, his breath catching. *A lady on the run,* he thought, a wry smile tugging at his lips. *What a mess you've made, Lady Tredegar.*

But his triumph at spotting her was short-lived. The 'ticking' in his head suddenly shifted. It wasn't a rhythmic pulse anymore; it was a sudden, violent strike, like a hammer hitting an anvil.

*Clang.*

His gaze drifted past Angharad, toward the cluster of men in the polished leather breastplates of the Watch, standing guard near the gangplank. And there, amidst the stern faces of the law, was a face that turned Tristan's blood to slush.

Cozme Aflor.

The man stood with a smug, effortless poise, his well-spoken manner evident even in the way he held his chin. A burn scar traced a jagged path near his ear, a pale mark against his tanned skin. He was laughing at something a fellow officer had said, his eyes bright and untroubled, looking every bit the successful man of the procession.

Tristan's fingers curled into his palms, his nails biting into the skin. Cozme. The first name on the List. The man who had stood in the shadows of a burning house while Tristan’s world turned to ash. The man who had taken everything and left only a boy and a vendetta.

"There," Tristan whispered, his voice trembling.

"There what?" Fortuna asked, suddenly upright, her golden eyes narrowing as she followed his gaze. Her boredom vanished, replaced by a sudden, sharp interest. "The odds just jumped, Tristan. They're leaping like salmon in a spring flood. Beautifully terrible."

The *Bluebell* groaned, a deep, wooden sound that vibrated in Tristan's very bones. The great white sails began to swell, catching a sudden, hungry gust of wind from the south. The ship began to pull away from the pier, the gap between the timber and the stone widening with agonizing slowness.

"Wait!" Tristan cried, though his voice was swallowed by the roar of the harbor.

He watched the gangplank begin to lift. He watched Angharad settle onto the deck, her eyes fixed on the horizon. And he watched Cozme Aflor, standing steady on the deck of the *Bluebell*, a man of the world, looking out at the sea as if he already owned it.

The ship was leaving. The ghost was boarding.

Tristan stood on the edge of the quay, the spray of the sea wetting his dark hair, his heart hammering a frantic rhythm against his ribs. He could stay here, in the safety of the Murk, among the familiar smells of dirt and old grease. Or he could leap into the chaos, chase the wind, and board the ship that carried his past and his future in its belly.

He looked at his hands—the unhurried, steady hands of a thief—and felt the cold, hard weight of the resolve settling in his gut. 

"Well?" Fortuna prodded, a mischievous, dangerous grin spreading across her face. "Are you a rat who stays in his hole, or a man who chases the moon?"

Tristan didn't answer. He simply lunged forward, running toward the moving ship, a predator closing in on his prize.
