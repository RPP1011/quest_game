The breathing was the first thing—heavy, rhythmic, the kind of sound a man makes when he thinks the world is small enough to belong to him. It came from the center of the room, a low, oceanic pull from the lungs of Yaotl Cuatzo. Each exhale was a slow landslide of air that seemed to vibrate through the floorboards and up into the soles of Tristan’s boots.

Tristan stayed low, a shadow among shadows, his belly nearly grazing the grit of the floor. He didn't move until the exhale peaked, then he drifted. He was a rat in the walls of the Azulejo, navigating the narrow gaps between the sleeping giant and the heavy oak of the cabinet.

Beside him, sprawled across a rafter that groaned under nothing but her own whims, Fortuna swung her legs. Her red dress was a smear of spilled wine against the dark wood, and her golden eyes caught the sliver of moonlight cutting through the high, narrow window.

"The odds are leaning left, Tristan," she whispered, her voice a theatrical rasp that only he could catch. She leaned down, her chin hovering a hair's breadth from his shoulder, though she cast no shadow. "He’s a heavy sleeper, but he’s got the scent of a man who’s used to waking up to a blade in his ribs. Don't get greedy."

Tristan didn't answer. He didn't have the breath to spare. He reached for the dosing kit at his belt, his fingers finding the small, cool vial of Spinster's Milk. He moved with the unhurried grace of a man counting coins, his thumb tracing the rim of the vial. The ticking in his head—the frantic, internal tally of Fortuna’s luck—was loud, a staccato rhythm that pulsed against his ribs. *Three to one on the silence,* he thought. *Five to one if the wood groans.*

He slid his hand toward the cabinet. It was dark wood, scarred by travel, standing like a silent sentinel near the edge of the bed. His fingers found the lock—a brass mechanism, tarnished but well-oiled. He drew a slender piece of steel from his kit, the metal biting cold against his calloused skin. 

He felt the first vibration of the lock against the tip of his tool. A tiny, stubborn resistance. He held his breath, his hands a mask of marble-stillness, while inside, the goddess began to pout.



The tumbler gave a soft, oily click, and Tristan’s thumb felt the victory before his eyes saw it. He didn't smile—smiling was for men who weren't currently stealing from the Azulejo hostel—but the ticking in his head slowed to a steady, satisfied hum. The cabinet door swung wide on well-greased hinges, exhaling the dry, dusty scent of old cedar and something sharper, like dried citrus peel.

Inside, the spoils waited. A heavy pouch of copper bits, a silver comb etched with swirling waves, and a small, lacquered box that hummed with the weight of something precious. Tristan’s fingers, unhurried and light as a spider's legs, reached for the pouch.

"Don't get greedy, *tia*," Fortuna bellowed. She was sprawled upside down in the air just above the bed, her molten-gold hair dangling toward the floor like weeping willow branches. Her red dress billowed in a wind that didn't exist in the cramped, dim room. "The lock was a gift, but the shadow in the hallway is a debt. And you know how she likes to collect."

Tristan ignored her, though the goddess's pout was a physical weight against his ribs. He slid the copper into his belt, the metal cool and reassuring against his thigh. He moved to the lacquered box, his eyes scanning the room for anything else—a stray coin, a discarded ring, anything to tip the odds of his night's take.

"The box," Fortuna whispered, her voice suddenly dropping its theatrical edge, sliding into a low, golden vibration. She leaned down, her face inches from his. "It has a heartbeat, Tristan. A slow, heavy one. Take it, and we'll eat like infanzones for a week."

He reached for the box. His fingers hovered just above the lid. *The Law of Rats says: take what is loose, but watch what is heavy,* he thought. The box was heavy. Very heavy.

"Just a little more," he murmured to himself, his voice a mere rasp in the stillness of the third watch. 

As his fingertips brushed the lacquer, a sound drifted through the thin walls of the hostel—the heavy, rhythmic thud of boots on the stairs. Not the soft shuffle of a servant, but the measured, purposeful stride of someone who owned the floor they walked upon.

Tristan froze. The ticking in his head spiked, a frantic, uneven beat. *A bad draw,* he thought. *A sudden change in the weather.*

"He's coming!" Fortuna hissed, flipping herself upright and scrambling to sit on the edge of the bed, her eyes wide and glowing. "The shadow is here, and he's bringing more than just a grudge!"
