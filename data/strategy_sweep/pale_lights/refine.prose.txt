The air in the Azulejo hostel tasted of old grease and the heavy, salt-damp breath of a sleeper. Tristan moved through the dark, not as a man, but as a shadow looking for a place to settle. His boots, worn soft as a beggar's prayer, met the floorboards with nothing more than a ghost of a sound.

*Easy, rat,* he thought. *The house is high, the odds are long, but the pot is sweet.*

In his head, the ticking began. It wasn't a clock; it was the frantic, uneven rhythm of Fortuna's whims, a mental tally of how many ways this could go sideways. To his left, sprawled precariously across a ceiling beam, Fortuna watched him with molten gold eyes. She looked bored, her red dress bunched around her hips as she dangled one leg over the wood.

"Don't trip on your own feet, Tristan," she whispered, her voice a velvet rasp that only he could hear. "The big one is breathing like a dying bull. One wrong vibration and he’ll have your head for a dice-pot."

Tristan ignored her. He focused on the cabinet. It sat in the corner, a squat thing of dark wood and iron bands, looking far too heavy for a man of Tristan’s slight frame to move quietly. But he wasn't moving the cabinet; he was moving the lock.

He knelt, his fingers finding the familiar grooves of his tools. The metal was cold, biting into his skin. Beside the cabinet, the man—Yaotl—shifted. The bedframe groaned, a low, wooden protest that seemed loud enough to shake the walls. Tristan froze. He held his breath until his lungs burned, his eyes fixed on the broad, dark shape under the black cloak. Yaotl’s breathing was deep, a rhythmic, heavy heave that spoke of a man used to the weight of armor, even in sleep.

*The odds are slipping,* the ticking in Tristan's head warned. *A bad beat is coming.*

"He’s waking," Fortuna pouted, leaning forward so far she nearly tumbled from the rafter. "Look at his hand. He's twitching. Do something, you little thief."

Tristan reached into his pouch and pinched a single, viscous drop of Spinster's Milk. He pressed the liquid to his tongue; it was cool, slightly bitter, and worked instantly to steady the frantic drumming of his heart. The world slowed. The tremor in his hands died.

He slid the tension wrench into the keyway. *Click.* A tiny sound, no louder than a beetle’s footfall, but in the silence of the room, it felt like a hammer strike. 

Yaotl let out a long, guttural sigh, his hand moving toward the hilt of the arming sword resting atop a trunk. Tristan didn't wait for the man to fully cross the line from sleep to wakefulness. He worked the pick with a surgeon's grace, feeling for the tumblers, dancing through the mechanism. 

*Click. Click.*

The lock yielded. The cabinet door creaked open—a microscopic sliver of movement—and Tristan slid his hand inside, his fingers brushing against the crisp, dry edges of parchment. He grabbed the bundle, pulling it close to his chest, his eyes never leaving the dark silhouette of the sleeper. 

For a heartbeat, the room held its breath. Yaotl groaned, a deep, rumbling sound in his chest, and then—silence. The rhythm of the breath returned, heavy and steady once more.

"Quick now," Fortuna hissed, her golden eyes dancing. "Before the luck runs out!"

Tristan didn't need telling twice. He tucked the papers into his tunic, kept his weight on the balls of his feet, and began the slow, agonizing retreat back toward the window. The papers were heavy against his ribs, a prize won on a razor's edge.
