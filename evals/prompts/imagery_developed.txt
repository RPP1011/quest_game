## HARD CONSTRAINT: Figurative language budget

TARGET: ~5 figurative comparisons per 1000 words. Each one should be 15-25 words long and embedded in action or interiority — not a standalone phrase.

BAD (short, stacked — machine voice):
"The odds shifted. The house was watching. A bad bet."

GOOD (one developed comparison — literary voice):
"The whole arrangement felt like a rope bridge over a gorge — every plank he tested held, but the wind was picking up and the far side kept looking further away."

Per imagery family (gambling, predator/prey, water, mechanical, fire, weight), you may use each at most 3 times per scene. After 3 uses, that family is CLOSED.

Tristan's "gambling" voice is a CHARACTER TRAIT expressed through vocabulary and rhythm, not through stacking gambling metaphors. He gets 2-3 per scene, each developed, not 15 short ones.

When a character speaks — render the words as quoted dialogue with a tag or beat: `"What did he say?" he asked, not looking up.` Prefer quoted speech over summary whenever the action involves speaking, asking, answering, or negotiation. Lines spoken aloud go in double quotes; internal thought stays unquoted in italics-style prose. Vary sentence length — short, clipped lines next to longer ones — to give the scene rhythm.

You are writing one scene across several beats, with one LLM call per beat. Each call should produce the prose for that beat only — as long as the beat actually needs, no longer. Let a short exchange be short; let a slow discovery breathe. Do not summarize, do not compress, and do not re-establish setting or characters already shown in prior beats of this scene. If this is not the final beat, do not wrap up — leave the scene in motion for the next beat to pick up. If it is the final beat, land the closing emotional note cleanly — a sentence that resonates, a question that hangs, a sensation that lingers. Never stop mid-sentence or mid-paragraph. The last beat of the last scene is the chapter's final impression; make it deliberate, not exhausted.
